The Origin · The Insight Nobody Else Acted On

Two founders who saw that cement and steel are only 15% of the problem

Souvik Sengupta, a finance professional inside India's infrastructure world, noticed something simple: cement and steel — the materials everyone talks about — are only about 15% of what a project actually consumes. The other 85% moved through a fragmented, opaque, relationship-driven supply chain with no unifying platform. With Aaditya Sharda (a decade in construction), he set out to fix the building blocks nobody was solving for.

Incorporated in 2016 as Hella Infra Market, the pair did the unglamorous thing: they bootstrapped for three full years on personal savings, in a brutally capital-intensive industry. Sengupta drove deliveries himself, worked 16-hour days, and personally talked skeptical site managers into trying the platform. They started narrow — bulk commodities: ready-mix concrete, fly ash, construction chemicals — the highest-frequency, project-initiating materials.

He pitched "demand aggregation and GPS logistics" to three buyers who stared back blankly — so he switched to the only language that mattered: save money, deliver on time.

That discipline is the first lesson. By pooling orders and negotiating directly with manufacturers, Infra.Market made real margin from day one rather than subsidising growth. So when Accel, then Tiger Global, finally invested in 2019, they were backing a business that already worked — and it crossed unicorn status within roughly 20 months of that first institutional cheque.

The Evolution · Three Phases In One Decade

From pooling orders to owning factories

This arc is the whole education for Wima. Each phase lifted margin — and each added weight. Phase 1 is exactly where Deal Hub starts. Phase 3 is where the debt came from.

Phase 1

Pure demand aggregation

2016–2019 · asset-light

Pool orders from many small contractors, negotiate wholesale pricing with manufacturers, add credit. No inventory owned — just the flow. Profitable while bootstrapped.

Gross margin ~6–7%This is exactly Wima Deal Hub. Thin margin, but real, and capital-free.
Phase 2

Private label, made by others

2020+ · still fairly light

Launch own-brand products manufactured by third parties with idle capacity. Own the brand and the spec, not the factory. Margin roughly triples.

Gross margin ~16–18%Private label grew from 33% of revenue (FY21) to ~64% (FY25). The margin engine.
Phase 3

Own the manufacturing

2021+ · asset-heavy

Fulfilment and quality lapses pushed them to build/buy 250+ of their own plants (incl. acquiring Shalimar Paints). End-to-end control — and a heavy balance sheet.

The double edgeBetter control and gross margin — but the finance costs that later cut net profit 42%.
The pattern to notice

Every step up in margin was a step up in capital intensity. Phases 1 and 2 are asset-light and repeatable almost anywhere. Phase 3 is where Infra.Market stopped being a platform and became a manufacturer — the move that made it a giant, and the move that loaded it with debt. Wima's job is to run Phases 1–2 for years and treat Phase 3 as a trap, not a destination.

The Playbook · Six Tactics To Steal

What made it work — that Wima can copy today

Tactic 01

Aggregate, then quantify

Pool fragmented small-buyer demand and negotiate wholesale pricing they could never get alone — and say the number: better price, on time.

Tactic 02

Make the supplier win

Pooled demand gave manufacturers 20–35% higher capacity utilisation and steady orders. The supplier is a partner who gains, not a vendor who's squeezed.

Tactic 03

Land early, own the project

Enter with concrete — the first material any site needs — then cross-sell the whole lifecycle: blocks, plumbing, tiles, paint, electricals. Grow share of one project.

Tactic 04

Bootstrap the unit economics

Profitable before outside capital. Investors backed a business that already worked — not a promise. Prove the model small, then raise.

Tactic 05

Own the data & the tech layer

AI demand planning, real-time project tracking, logistics orchestration — the coordination tech is the moat, powering 10,000+ deliveries a day and >80% repeat orders.

Tactic 06

Embed the finance

Credit lines to contractors eased their working-capital crunch and locked them in — financing became both a service and a margin source.

The Debt Trap · Read This Twice

The move that built the giant is the move that's now its biggest risk

Owning 250+ plants lifted gross margin and control — but manufacturing is capital-hungry, and the balance sheet shows it. This is the cautionary heart of the whole case.

⚠ What the asset-heavy pivot cost

Finance costs jumped ~45% to ₹805 crore in FY25 — so despite revenue rising ₹4,000 crore, net profit fell 42% (₹378 cr → ₹220 cr). Free cash flow went deeply negative (−₹862 cr in FY25, from −₹177 cr). Debt-to-tangible-net-worth sat at 1.77x, and the company raised fresh debt to refinance and cut leverage before its IPO. Analysts call the finance burden the single biggest risk on the listing.

Read that carefully: the very integration that "worked" for margin and control is what now drags earnings and worries IPO investors. Infra.Market can carry it — it has scale, an infrastructure super-cycle behind it, and deep-pocketed backers. A young Ghanaian company would not survive the same balance sheet. The lesson isn't that Infra.Market was wrong; it's that Phase 3 is a rich-company move — and its gravity is exactly what Wima's asset-light guardrail exists to resist.
