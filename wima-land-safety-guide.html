Buying Land in Ghana — How Not to Lose Your Money

Land disputes are among the most common cases in Ghana's courts, and the single biggest fear for anyone — especially the diaspora — building back home. This guide walks the verification and registration process in plain language, so you know what to check before you pay. It applies to any purchase, anywhere in Ghana.

Read this first — this is guidance, not legal advice. Land law is complex and changes, and every parcel is different. Nothing here replaces a licensed lawyer and a licensed surveyor. Always confirm current requirements directly with the Lands Commission and take independent legal counsel before paying for any land. Use this guide to ask better questions — not as your only source.
The one rule that prevents most disasters

Never pay in full before the searches are complete. Most people who lose money on land in Ghana paid a large sum on the strength of an indenture alone — a document that proves someone sold you land, but not that they owned it. Verify first. Pay in stages. That single discipline avoids the most common trap.

The distinction that catches everyone: indenture vs title

An indenture (or deed of assignment) is a sale contract. It shows a transfer happened — but it does not prove the seller had the legal right to sell. Multiple people can hold indentures for the same plot. This is exactly how the same land gets "sold" several times.

A registered Land Title Certificate, issued by the Lands Commission, is government-backed proof of legal ownership — the highest protection available, and what courts, banks and mortgage lenders recognise. Since September 2025, lenders require it for any property financing. Convert an indenture to a registered title as soon as you can.

The verification & registration path

Roughly nine steps, from checking the land to holding a registered title. Some order and detail varies by region and by whether it's a first registration or a transfer — your lawyer confirms the exact path.
  1. Do the search before anything elseConduct an official land search at the Lands Commission using the site plan and indenture, to confirm current ownership and reveal any disputes, encumbrances or litigation on the land.
    You + your lawyer · before paying
  2. Verify the seller and the root of titleTrace the land back to the original allodial (stool, skin, or family) owner. For stool land, that means the allocation note and stool documents; for family land, a signed family resolution from authorised representatives.
    Your lawyer
  3. Commission an independent site surveyA licensed surveyor produces a certified, geo-referenced site plan with a Lands Commission barcode. Since September 2025 geo-referenced plans are required, and satellite verification is used to confirm boundaries.
    Licensed surveyor — mandatory
  4. Execute the indenture / deed with your lawyerThe legal instrument conveying the interest is drawn up and properly witnessed. Use your own independent lawyer — never the seller's.
    Your lawyer
  5. Pay stamp duty to the GRAStamp duty is paid to the Ghana Revenue Authority; the receipt must accompany the indenture. Registration is refused without it.
    You · GRA
  6. Submit the registration applicationLodge the application at the Lands Commission (online via its portal or in person) with the full document set — ID/Ghana Card, TINs, root of title, stamped indenture, certified site plan.
    You + your lawyer · Lands Commission
  7. Examination & assessmentThe Lands Commission examines the application and assesses it. Incomplete applications are the most common cause of rejection and delay — assemble everything before submitting.
    Lands Commission
  8. Publication / notification periodOn the first-registration pathway there is a public notification period, allowing any competing claim to surface before title is granted.
    Lands Commission
  9. Pay fees & receive your Title CertificateSettle the Lands Commission fees and registration levy; once approved, the registered Land Title Certificate is issued — your government-backed proof of ownership.
    You · Lands Commission
How long & roughly how much? Timelines vary widely by region and completeness — commonly quoted at anywhere from about 1 to 6 months, longer where records are contested or documents incomplete. On professional fees, published guides suggest budgeting broadly in the region of GHS 1,500–5,000 for legal work and GHS 2,000–8,000 for survey work depending on land size and complexity, plus statutory stamp duty and Lands Commission fees that scale with the land's value. Treat all figures as indicative — confirm current fees with the Lands Commission and quotes from your own professionals.

⚑ Walk-away warning signs

  • "Just pay, the indenture is enough." It isn't. An unregistered indenture is a sale contract, not ownership.
  • Pressure to pay in full before searches finish. The single most common way money is lost.
  • The seller offers you their lawyer. Always get your own independent legal counsel.
  • No geo-referenced, barcoded site plan from a licensed surveyor — or boundaries that don't match the ground.
  • Seller won't produce the root of title (stool/family/state grant tracing back to the allodial owner).
  • Land guards involved. Land-guard activity is a serious red flag — and their violent conduct is criminalised under the Land Act 2020.
  • A price far below the area's norm. On land, cheap very often means contested, encumbered, or not the seller's to sell.
Diaspora — the extra layer. Managing this from abroad multiplies the risk, because you can't stand in the office or on the plot. Appoint an independent lawyer (not introduced by the seller), insist on the official Lands Commission search before any transfer, ask for dated photos of the plot and the documents, and release money only in stages against verified milestones. The same discipline that protects a building project protects a land purchase: verify, document, pay in stages.
