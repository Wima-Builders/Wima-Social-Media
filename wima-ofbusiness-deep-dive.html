The Head-To-Head · The Argument Settled

Two companies, one market, opposite answers

Both aggregate construction materials demand in India. Both started within a year of each other. Both are IPO-bound. One bought 250+ factories; one stayed light and lent money instead. Here is what FY25 says about who was right.

The punchline

OfBusiness earns 2.7× the profit on higher revenue — and the market prices it at roughly two to three times Infra.Market's valuation. Not because it out-sold its rival, but because it refused to convert coordination into concrete. It never took on the asset base whose finance costs cut Infra.Market's profit by 42% in a record revenue year. Same thesis, lighter body, better outcome.

Origin · The Insight

An investor who noticed that credit and commerce are the same problem

Asish Mohapatra was a venture capitalist at Matrix Partners looking at India's SMEs. Ruchi Kalra was at McKinsey. In 2015 they founded OfBusiness in Gurugram with Bhuvan Gupta, Vasant Sridhar and others, around a mission they stated plainly: democratise access to raw materials and credit for MSMEs. Note that "and credit" — it was in the founding sentence, not bolted on later.

The market was enormous and underserved: India's 63.4 million SMEs employ ~460 million people and generate ~30% of GDP — yet traditional banks barely lend to them. Small manufacturers and contractors were squeezed twice: they paid retail prices for raw materials because they couldn't order at volume, and they couldn't get working capital to buy at the right moment.

"As we explored the B2B value chains, we understood that credit and commerce went hand in hand. So we could either be a commerce company solving credit through partnerships — or build the credit play ourselves and capture the profit pool."

That sentence, from Oxyzo's Prashant Roy Sharma, is the fork in the road — and OfBusiness took the second path. They built the lender. In 2018 the purchase-financing arm was carved out as Oxyzo Financial Services, an RBI-registered NBFC led by Ruchi Kalra. In March 2022 Oxyzo raised $200M in India's largest-ever Series A (Alpha Wave, Tiger Global, Norwest), becoming a unicorn in its own right — the only woman-led Indian startup to do so that year. Its loan book has grown past ₹5,000 crore.

The parent hit unicorn status in July 2021 and has raised ~$776M across 15 rounds. But the structural point is what matters for Wima: the financing arm didn't just support the commerce — it became a business worth a billion dollars by itself, and roughly 70% of Oxyzo's lending now goes to businesses beyond OfBusiness's own platform.

The Model · Three Engines, One Flywheel

Commerce brings them in. Credit makes the money. Tenders bring them back.

This is the architecture Wima should study hardest — because it makes real money without owning a single asset.

Engine 01 · The traffic

Raw material procurement

Thin margin · builds the relationship

Steel, cement, chemicals, polymers, construction materials across 9 industries. Verified suppliers, transparent prices, logistics enabled. It aggregates SME demand to unlock pricing none could get alone.

The roleMaterials are the front door, not the profit. They create the transaction — and the data.
Engine 02 · The margin

Oxyzo — embedded credit

The real profit pool

Working capital lending matched to the cash-flow cycle of buying raw materials. Because OfBusiness sees every transaction, it underwrites on real behavioural data — approving in under 48 hours with materially lower default rates than banks.

The roleThis is where the money is. Banks can't underwrite these SMEs; OfBusiness can, because it owns the data.
Engine 03 · The loop

BidAssist — tender discovery

Free utility that feeds everything

Launched 2017: aggregates and recommends government tenders to SMEs — 200,000+ tender sources across 190+ countries. Free to use, premium subscription available. It hands small builders work they'd never have found.

The roleThe genius move. Win them a contract → finance it → supply it. See the flywheel below.

The Flywheel · The Single Best Idea In This Document

Give them the job. Fund the job. Supply the job.

BidAssist looks like a free tool. It is actually the top of a self-reinforcing loop that competitors can't easily copy — and it maps almost perfectly onto Wima's planned tendering desk.

Step 01

Find them work

A small builder discovers a tender through BidAssist they'd never have known existed. Pure value, no ask.

Step 02

Fund the win

They win it — but need working capital to buy materials. Oxyzo lends, underwritten on their platform history.

Step 03

Supply the job

OfBusiness supplies the raw materials for the contract it just helped them win and finance.

Step 04

Own the data

Every order sharpens the credit model, which prices the next loan better — which wins the next customer.

Why this is the lesson for Wima

Wima already has a tendering desk on the roadmap and a bank rail in the Bank Partnership Pitch — this is how they connect. Aggregate Ghana's tenders (government, estate developers, institutional) into something a small Accra builder can actually find and understand. Hand them work. Then be the one who helps them finance and supply it. The builder who got their contract from Wima does not shop around for boards. That's a moat no supplier can price-match — and it costs nothing but the aggregation.

The Numbers · Where It Stands

Profitable, IPO-bound, and priced like a fintech

The group

OfBusiness

FY25 revenue ₹22,241 cr · PAT ₹597 cr. Founded 2015. ~$776M raised over 15 rounds. 1M+ SMEs served, 500,000+ orders, 30+ countries. Five banks mandated (Axis, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, Citi, BofA) for a $750M–$1B IPO at a $6–9B target valuation.

The engine

Oxyzo

RBI-registered NBFC, carved out 2018, led by Ruchi Kalra. $200M Series A in 2022 — India's largest — unicorn at $1B. Loan book past ₹5,000 cr; AUM ₹5,000 cr+. Underwrites in under 48 hours on platform data. ~70% of its lending is now outside OfBusiness — it outgrew its parent's platform.

The tell

Look at what the market is pricing. Infra.Market — bigger brand, more factories, more physical control — is valued around $2.6B. OfBusiness, which owns comparatively little, is targeting $6–9B. Investors are paying a premium for the asset-light, data-and-credit model over the vertically integrated one. In this market, owning less is worth more.

The Wima Translation · Tap Each Lesson

What Wima copies — and where Wima must diverge

OfBusiness is closer to Wima's reality than Infra.Market ever was: it made money without capital-heavy assets. But Wima is a young Ghanaian company, not a $776M-funded Indian group — so some moves need translating down, and one needs refusing outright.

The one line to hold

Infra.Market proved the aggregation thesis and then bought the factories that ate its profit. OfBusiness proved the same thesis, refused the factories, and made its money on the flow — and earns 2.7× the profit for it. Wima copies OfBusiness's shape, not Infra.Market's ambition.
