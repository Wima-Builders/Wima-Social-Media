The Thesis In One Screen

Why aggregation can work — and where it quietly kills you

Ghana's building-materials market is fragmented, offline, and priced opaquely — the exact condition aggregation platforms exist to fix. Pool many small builders' orders, and the combined volume unlocks prices none of them could get alone. That part is proven. The danger is how you do it.

The upside, proven

Demand aggregation is real and large. Infra.Market and OfBusiness turned pooled construction demand into multi-billion businesses in a market like ours; Jumba is doing it across Kenya right now. Suppliers gain steady volume; builders save 5–10%. The model works.

The trap, just as proven

Groupon built the same "aggregate demand → unlock discounts → take a cut" model to a $16B IPO — then lost it all, because deal-hunters never became loyal and squeezed merchants fled. The mechanic that grows you can be the mechanic that kills you.

Four platforms that made aggregation pay

Pooled hundreds of contractors' orders to unlock wholesale pricing, gave manufacturers steady demand, and rode a flywheel: more buyers → more leverage → lower prices → more buyers.

Steal thisAggregation-first, with a quantified saving ("buy together, save 5–10%") and a supplier promise of steady volume. Sell the pool, not a discount.

OfBusiness

India · materials + finance

Ran leaner on assets than Infra.Market and made its real margin from embedded financing — lending to the builders buying materials — rather than the thin materials markup itself.

Steal thisDon't chase margin on the materials. Monetise the flow — financing (bank rail), installation, verification, data — while materials build the traffic and trust.

Connects manufacturers to hardware stores and developers, negotiates discounts plus a markup, and adds retailer financing — solving the exact "fluctuating prices and random stockouts" pain Ghana shares. But it runs in-house logistics, which adds capital weight.

Steal thisThe African playbook is validated: standardised pricing, one-stop sourcing, retailer finance. But note where Jumba took on capital — and don't copy that part.

Team-buy

Turned group-buying into a social, recurring habit — you rally friends to unlock a price — rather than a one-off discount voucher. The pooling itself is the product, and it repeats.

Steal thisMake the group-buy social and recurring (a standing community that pools every cycle), not a one-time coupon. Recurring pooling builds the habit Groupon never did.

The Wreckage · Read This Twice

Four ways the same model went to zero

Groupon

the group-deal trap
The most important warning for a "Deal Hub"

Built "aggregate demand → unlock discounts → take a cut" to a $16B IPO, then lost ~$1.14B. The model attracted deal-tourists loyal to the discount, not the business; merchants often lost money on deals and stopped signing up — a death spiral. As the trade saying goes: if they come for price, they leave for price.

The warningA platform built on discounts trains price-hunters and squeezes suppliers. Deals cannot be the foundation — only a rail on top of real value.

Copia

Kenya · B2B commerce
Capital intensity + over-expansion

Raised over $100M, ran its own logistics and warehousing, expanded fast — and collapsed into administration in 2024 when the capital ran out and the macro turned. Peers downsized in the same Kenyan cash crunch.

The warningOwning logistics and burning capital to grow works only until funding or the macro turns. Fixed costs are fatal in a downturn.

BuildDirect

US · building materials e-commerce
Inventory is a trap

A building-materials marketplace that took on inventory and warehousing to guarantee supply, over-expanded, and filed for creditor protection in 2017 — despite huge funding. The asset weight, not the demand, sank it.

The warningThe pressure to "guarantee supply" pulls you toward owning inventory. That's the moment materials aggregation stops being asset-light.

Infra.Market (the debt chapter)

India · the same winner
Even the champion got bitten

The aggregation star moved into owning manufacturing and private-label factories. Margins thinned (~8% EBITDA), debt piled up, and it took a credit-rating downgrade in 2025 — while its asset-lighter rival kept higher margins.

The warningSuccess tempts you to own the assets you once merely coordinated. That pivot is exactly where the margin and the freedom disappear.

The Verdict · Guardrails To Hold

Deal Hub is viable — if these five lines never move

The five non-negotiables

1 · Value first, deals second — Deal Hub rides on a community earned with content, never a discount engine that stands alone. 2 · Asset-light, always — coordinate orders and logistics; never own inventory, warehouses or factories. 3 · Suppliers must win too — aggregation gives them steady volume and faster payment, never a squeeze. 4 · Monetise the flow, not the markup — installation, finance, verification and data are the margin; materials are the traffic. 5 · Sequence, don't sprawl — Drywall with Jamal first, Accra first; prove, then widen.

The one line to hold

Groupon proves a deal is not a business. Wima Deal Hub only works because the deal sits on top of trust Wima already earned — the aggregation saves real money, the suppliers genuinely win, and Wima never owns the thing that sinks everyone else: the inventory.
