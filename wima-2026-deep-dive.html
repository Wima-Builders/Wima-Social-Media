Finding 1 · The Macro Window

2026 is a narrow, real opening — move with it

After years of pressure, the fundamentals turned: inflation into single digits, the cedi recovering, default exited, the IMF programme completing May 2026, and consecutive rate cuts. This is the best financing environment in a decade — but analysts are clear it's a narrow window, not a new normal.

Why it matters

Finance rails come alive

Falling rates and competing banks revive mortgage and construction lending — Wima's bank-referral rail gets its best-ever conditions. Timing matters.

Why it matters

Diaspora entry point

A stronger cedi and easing rates make 2026 one of the most favourable diaspora entry points in memory — the audience with USD is ready to move.

The risk

It can reverse

A renewed inflation spike, sharp cedi depreciation, or an IMF-exit wobble could choke the recovery. Build asset-light so a reversal can't sink you.

Finding 2 · The Defining Paradox

1.8 million short — and 1.3 million empty, at the same time

This single fact reframes the whole opportunity. Ghana doesn't simply lack houses — it builds them at the wrong price point.

What it means for Wima

The money and the demand are in affordable and mid-market ($40K–$150K), where sub-$150K gated units sell in under 45 days while $600K+ villas sit 90+ days. Two sharp implications: (1) Wima's self-builder audience is exactly the under-served majority — the strategy is aimed at the right people. (2) Those 1.3M empty units are a short-let and management goldmine — inventory that already exists, waiting to be activated.

Infra.Market fixed India's fragmented construction supply chain — the same coordination, standardisation and trust problem Ghana has. Its entry point was pure demand aggregation: pool the orders of hundreds of small contractors, and the pooled volume unlocks pricing individuals can't get — saving buyers 5–7% while giving manufacturers steady demand and fuller capacity. It stayed asset-light early, added quality standardisation and private labels later, and rode a flywheel: more buyers → more supplier leverage → lower prices → more buyers.

What Wima copiesRun Deal Hub as demand aggregation first — pool builders' orders, quantify the saving (\u201cbuy together, save 5–10%\u201d), keep suppliers happy with steady volume. Own the flow and the data; add private-label or deeper integration only much later.
The paired warning

Infra.Market's move into owning manufacturing brought thinner margins (~8% EBITDA), heavy debt, and a 2025 credit downgrade — while its asset-light rival kept higher margins. Same lesson as Zillow's iBuying loss: aggregate and own the flow; be very slow to own physical assets.
