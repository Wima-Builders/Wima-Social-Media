The Honest BOQ Checklist

A Bill of Quantities (BOQ) is the itemised list of every material and labour cost in your building project. This one page is how you check whether the quote in your hand is honest — before you pay a pesewa. Works for any contractor, any project. Not just Wima.

Why this matters: in Ghana, most quotes are given verbally, with no breakdown — which is exactly how the final bill ends up 20–30% higher than the number you agreed. An honest quote has nothing to hide, so it itemises everything. If a contractor won't put these lines in writing, that tells you what you need to know.

1 Materials — every item, named

Each material listed by name, brand/grade, quantity and unit price — not one lumped "materials" figure.
  • Every material named individually — e.g. "12mm plasterboard," not just "boards." Vague names hide cheap substitutions.
  • Brand or grade stated for each item. "Acoustic tile" ranges from GHS 40 to GHS 220/m² — the grade is the price.
  • Quantity and unit shown (m², bags, sheets, lengths) so you can check the maths yourself.
  • Unit price per item — the cost of one, so you can compare against the market.
  • Wastage/offcuts noted if added (usually 5–10%) — not hidden inside inflated quantities.

2 Labour — separate from materials

Labour costed on its own line. If materials and labour are merged, you can't tell what either really costs.
  • Labour shown separately from materials — its own figure, ideally per m² or per stage.
  • What the labour covers — installation only, or also delivery, prep, and clean-up?
  • Number of days / crew size if the job is priced by time.

3 Scope & timeline — in writing

What's included, what's not, and when it happens — so "that wasn't in the price" can't appear later.
  • Exact scope — the rooms, the areas (m²), what's being done to each.
  • What's excluded stated plainly — painting, electrical, making-good, disposal.
  • Start date and duration in writing, with what causes a delay.
  • Who supplies what — do you buy any materials, or does the contractor supply all?

4 Payment & protection

How and when money moves — the single biggest place clients lose leverage.
  • Staged payments tied to progress — never a large sum before work starts. A fair split is a deposit, a payment on delivery, and a balance on completion.
  • What each payment unlocks — e.g. "50% on materials delivered to site."
  • A guarantee / after-care period stated — what happens if something fails after install.
  • Total in figures and words, dated, with the contractor's name and contact — a document, not a text message.
  • For diaspora: dated photos promised at delivery, install start, and completion; and a clean payment method (Wise / bank transfer) with written receipt.

⚑ Walk-away red flags

  • "I'll give you a price on site" with nothing written down.
  • One lump "materials" figure with no breakdown — the classic place the extra 30% hides.
  • Wants most of the money up front, before materials are even on site.
  • Won't name brands or grades — "don't worry, it's good quality" is not a specification.
  • No written timeline, no start date, no end date.
  • The cheapest quote by far — it usually means something's been left out that you'll pay for later.
