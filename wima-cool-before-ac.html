How old Accra homes stayed cool — without AC

Before air-conditioning, Ghana's builders kept homes comfortable with design alone — high ceilings, cross-ventilation, deep eaves, shade. Those ideas still work, and they cost you nothing to run. Here's what they knew, what the research says, and how to bring it into a modern home.

Walk through an old bungalow in Cantonments, Ridge or Adabraka and you'll notice it feels cooler than a brand-new build down the road — even with no air-conditioning running. That isn't nostalgia. It's engineering. Those homes were designed for the climate, in an era when running cost mattered and AC wasn't an option. Modern builds often abandoned those features and bolted on air-conditioning instead — which is a large part of why the ECG bill hurts.

What the old builders knew

A 2026 systematic review of passive cooling in tropical climates found that combining these strategies consistently outperforms any single one — and that cross-ventilation and shading lead in hot-humid conditions like Accra's.
  1. High ceilingsHeat rises. Tall rooms let the hottest air collect well above where people sit and sleep, keeping the living zone cooler. It's why those old rooms feel airy the moment you step in.
  2. Cross-ventilationWindows and openings placed on opposite walls let a breeze pass right through the room and carry heat out with it — the single most effective passive strategy in a hot-humid climate. A closed, single-aspect room traps heat; a cross-ventilated one breathes.
  3. Deep eaves & verandasWide roof overhangs and shaded verandas stop direct sun from ever reaching the walls and windows — so the building never absorbs the heat in the first place. Shade is cheaper than cooling.
  4. Elevated floorsOlder homes were often raised off the ground, letting air move underneath and keeping damp and ground heat away from the living space.
  5. Roof & ceiling separationA ventilated roof space and a proper ceiling put a buffer of air between the hot roof and the rooms below — the principle a modern insulated or Cool-Ceiling system takes further.
The honest part: passive design does a lot, but it can't do everything in a modern Accra home — smaller plots, concrete roofs and glass-heavy facades all work against it, and you can't always add verandas or move windows to an existing building. That's where a modern ceiling system picks up where the old design left off.
